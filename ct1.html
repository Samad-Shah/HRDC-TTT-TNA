Learning, Training & Development
Three related but distinct concepts â€” know them precisely for the exam
Learning

The process of acquiring knowledge, skills, attitudes, or behaviours through study, experience, practice, or teaching. It involves a lasting change in an individual's understanding or ability. Not just memorising facts â€” it's developing the capacity to apply what is learned in real-life or work situations.

Training

The process of providing required competency to the employee for doing the job effectively, skillfully, and efficiently. Training is not continuous â€” it is periodical, given at a specified time by an expert trainer or professional. It bridges the performance gap between what the employee has and what the job demands.

Development

Supports broader personal and professional advancement beyond a specific job. While training focuses on the current role, development prepares individuals for future roles and higher responsibilities. It is long-term, ongoing, and tied to career growth and organisational capability.

How Adults Learn Differently
Adult learning principles guide trainers to design learning that is relevant, respectful, experiential, problem-focused, and learner-driven
Andragogy vs Pedagogy
Andragogy = the art and science of helping adults learn (Knowles). Pedagogy = teaching children. Adults are self-directed, experience-rich, and need to know why before they learn. Children are more dependent on the teacher's direction.
The 7 Adult Learning Principles
Apply these when designing and delivering any training for adult learners
1
Adults need to know WHY they are learning
Adults are more motivated when they understand the purpose and benefit of learning.
ðŸ’¡ At the start of training, explain how the topic will help them solve problems or improve results in their role.
2
Adults bring life experience and knowledge
Adults arrive with a wealth of prior knowledge, skills, and opinions that can enrich the learning process.
ðŸ’¡ Encourage sharing of examples, use group discussions, and link new knowledge to what they already know.
3
Adults are self-directed
Adults prefer to take ownership of their learning rather than being told exactly what to do at every step.
ðŸ’¡ Give options in learning activities and allow learners to set some of their own learning goals.
4
Adults are problem-centered
Adults learn best when content is presented in a way that helps them address real-life challenges.
ðŸ’¡ Use workplace case studies, simulations, or role plays that mirror actual problems they face.
5
Adults are motivated by internal factors
While external rewards (certificates, promotions) matter, internal motivators like personal growth, job satisfaction, and achievement are stronger.
ðŸ’¡ Show how the learning connects to personal success, mastery, or making a difference.
6
Adults need respect and a safe learning environment
Adults expect to be treated as equals, and they learn best when they feel psychologically safe.
ðŸ’¡ Acknowledge their experience, listen actively, and create a no-judgment space for questions and mistakes.
7
Adults benefit from practical, active learning
Adults retain knowledge better when they apply it immediately through action.
ðŸ’¡ Use hands-on exercises, projects, or real-time problem-solving instead of just lectures.
Learning Objectives vs Learning Outcomes
Often confused â€” understand the difference clearly
Participant's Learning Objective

A statement describing what a participant is expected to learn from the training. Set at the beginning â€” the trainer gathers participants' expectations and adjusts content accordingly. Gathered by asking: "What do you expect to learn?" or using note pads and flip charts.

Participant's Learning Outcomes

What the participant actually learns or can demonstrate after the training. It is the results or evidence of learning â€” measurable and observable. This is what the trainer evaluates at the end of the session.

American educator, "father of adult education." Distinguished andragogy (adult learning) from pedagogy (children's learning).

What is a Trainer?

Definition of a Trainer
A person who facilitates learning and development of others by designing, delivering, and evaluating training programs, using appropriate methods to build knowledge, skills, and attitudes that improve individual and organisational performance. What is Competency?
The combination of knowledge, skills, abilities, and behaviours that enable a person to perform a job or task successfully. A competent trainer must have the right training knowledge, skills, abilities, and behaviours to train effectively.

    Ethics Guide
    for Trainers

    Code of Ethics (CoE) defines the values and principles behind trainer behaviour. Code of Conduct (CoC) defines the specific rules and actions.

    Code of Ethics Â· Code of Conduct Â· Professionalism
    Ethics vs Conduct
    These two are often confused â€” know the distinction clearly
    Code of Ethics (CoE)

    A set of values and guiding principles that shape a trainer's professional behaviour. It's about the "why" and "how" a trainer upholds integrity in their role. Governs the internal compass â€” what the trainer believes and values.

    Code of Conduct (CoC)

    A set of specific rules and behaviours trainers are expected to follow. It's about "what" you do and "what actions" are acceptable or unacceptable. Governs the external behaviour â€” visible actions during training.

    4.1 Trainer's Code of Ethics (CoE)
    7 principles that define a trainer's professional values
    1
    Integrity & Honesty
    Be truthful, transparent, and avoid misleading learners or clients. Never fabricate results or misrepresent training outcomes.
    2
    Respect for Learners
    Value each participant's perspective, background, and learning pace. Treat all learners with dignity regardless of their level.
    3
    Confidentiality
    Protect sensitive learner or organisational information shared during training. Never disclose private information to third parties.
    4
    Commitment to Excellence
    Maintain high standards in training design, delivery, and assessment. Continuously improve your methods and outputs.
    5
    Continuous Learning
    Keep updating personal knowledge, skills, and professional practices. A trainer who stops learning cannot effectively guide others.
    6
    Fairness & Non-Discrimination
    Ensure equal opportunities for learning regardless of race, gender, age, or beliefs. Design and deliver inclusive training.
    7
    Social Responsibility
    Contribute to the betterment of the community and the training profession. Consider the wider impact of training on society.
    4.2 Trainer's Code of Conduct (CoC)
    9 specific rules and behaviours expected from every professional trainer
    1
    Punctuality
    Arrive on time and respect agreed training schedules. Starting late disrespects learners' time and sets a poor example.
    2
    Professional Appearance
    Dress appropriately for the training context. Appearance contributes to credibility and setting the right professional tone.
    3
    Preparedness
    Come fully ready with training materials, equipment, and content. An unprepared trainer is an ineffective trainer.
    4
    Use of Language
    Avoid offensive, discriminatory, or inappropriate language in all forms of communication during training.
    5
    Proper Use of Materials
    Give credit for content and avoid plagiarism. Never use copyrighted materials without permission or attribution.
    6
    Learner Engagement
    Actively involve all participants without bias or exclusion. Every learner deserves equal opportunity to participate.
    7
    Ethical Assessment Practices
    Evaluate learners fairly and honestly. Assessment results must reflect actual performance, not personal preferences.
    8
    Avoiding Conflicts of Interest
    Disclose and manage any potential conflicts. Personal interests must never compromise training quality or fairness.
    9
    Safety & Well-being
    Ensure a safe, comfortable learning environment at all times. Physical and psychological safety are the trainer's responsibility.
