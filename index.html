3 Levels of Data Analysis

TNA should be conducted at all 3 levels simultaneously ‚Äî they are interlinked for a balanced analysis.

1. Organisational

Big picture ‚Äî does training align with company strategy?

Where is training needed?
Focus Areas:
  • ‚Üí Mission, vision & long-term plans
  • ‚Üí External/internal factors (tech, regulations)
  • ‚Üí Short & long-term business objectives
  • ‚Üí Organisational culture, structure & system
Data Sources:

KPIs, business plans, senior management interviews, market trend reports

2. Departmental

Task/operational level ‚Äî what does the job require?

What must employees do?
Focus Areas:
  • ‚Üí Expected vs actual performance
  • ‚Üí KSAs needed for the job
  • ‚Üí Tools, processes, technology required
  • ‚Üí Right people in right roles
Data Sources:

Job analysis, SOPs, observation, supervisor/SME interviews

  • ‚Üí Current job performance

Data Gathering Methods

Use at least 2‚Äì3 methods together for a complete, well-rounded picture.

üí¨ Qualitative = Soft data (feelings, opinions)
üìä Quantitative = Hard data (numbers, %)
Interview (1-on-1)
Qualitative
Best for: Root cause, SMEs, personal insight
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Rich, descriptive detail
  • Consistent data to identify patterns
  • Fleshes out quantitative survey data
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Very time-consuming
  • Skewed if interviewees not representative
  • Risk of interpreting responses
Focus Groups
Qualitative
Best for: Shared issues, diverse perspectives
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Develops testable hypotheses
  • Facilitator notes nonverbal behaviors
  • All members heard, not just vocal ones
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Time- and resource-intensive
  • Dominant members may skew results
  • Hard for 1 facilitator to run + note-take
Observation
Qualitative
Best for: Physical skills, workflow, on-the-job
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Excellent for psychomotor skills
  • Creates step-by-step standard procedures
  • Identifies environment conditions
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Hard to identify task start/end
  • Misses mental processes
  • Hawthorne effect (act differently when watched)
Extant Data (Records)
Quantitative
Best for: Initial baseline, org/dept level
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Hard, objective data
  • Trends & patterns over time
  • No confidentiality issues (aggregate)
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Collected for other purposes
  • No control over collection method
  • Data requires "sifting"
Surveys & Questionnaires
Both
Best for: Large groups, broad training trends
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Inexpensive and fast
  • Easy for respondents
  • Provides both qual & quant data
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Hard to write clear, unambiguous questions
  • Respondents may answer blindly
  • Large sample needed for reliability
Tests & Assessments
Quantitative
Best for: Objective KSA validation
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Highly objective and consistent
  • Pinpoints specific K/S gaps
  • Focuses design on exact gap
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Doesn't reveal thought processes
  • Test anxiety may affect results
  • Hard to cover K & S both in limited time

Key Models

McGoldrick & Tobey's 7-Step Model + Gilbert's 6-Box performance analysis.

McGoldrick & Tobey

7-Step TNA Model ‚Äî training must be needs-driven, not assumption-driven

1

Define Purpose & Scope

Clarify why TNA is needed, what's in/out of scope.

2

Gather Background Information

Review existing records, reports, past training data.

3

Identify Stakeholders & Gain Support

Get buy-in from executives, managers, team leads.

4

Plan the Needs Assessment

Choose methods, assign roles, set timeline & budget.

5

Collect Data

Run surveys, interviews, focus groups, observations.

6

Analyze Data & Identify Gaps

Compare current vs desired. Prioritize by urgency.

7

Report Findings & Recommend

Present tables/charts; quick wins + long-term solutions.

Memory tip: "Dogs Gather In Packs, Chasing After Rabbits" (Define, Gather, Identify, Plan, Collect, Analyze, Report)
