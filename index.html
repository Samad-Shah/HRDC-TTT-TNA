Why Do a TNA? ‚Äî 9 Key Applications
Benefits of conducting a Training Needs Assessment before embarking on any training program
‚ë† Align with Business Goals

Ensures training directly supports organisational strategy, mission, and KPIs. Example: if company aims to improve customer satisfaction, TNA identifies the need for customer service training.

‚ë° Identify Performance Gaps

Distinguishes training-related issues (skill/knowledge gaps) from non-training issues (lack of resources, unclear processes, motivation problems).

‚ë¢ Design Relevant Training

Provides a foundation for developing practical, targeted, job-specific content. Avoids generic "one-size-fits-all" training by tailoring solutions to actual needs.

‚ë£ Efficient Use of Resources

Prevents wasted investment in unnecessary or misaligned training. Ensures training budgets are spent where they add the most value.

‚ë§ Employee Development & Career

Identifies skill gaps for promotions, succession planning, and career pathways. Encourages a culture of continuous learning.

‚ë• Change Management

Helps employees adapt to new systems, technology, or business processes. Example: Before implementing a new ERP system, TNA identifies training required for smooth adoption.

‚ë¶ Compliance & Risk Management

Ensures employees receive required regulatory or safety training. Reduces legal, safety, and operational risks.

‚ëß Improve Engagement & Retention

When training is relevant, employees feel valued and supported in their roles. Boosts motivation, confidence, and loyalty.

‚ë® Evaluate Training Effectiveness

Provides benchmarks to measure whether training interventions actually closed performance gaps. Feeds into models like Kirkpatrick's evaluation framework.

3 Levels of Data Analysis

TNA should be conducted at all 3 levels simultaneously ‚Äî they are interlinked for a balanced analysis.

1. Organisational

Big picture ‚Äî does training align with company strategy?

Where is training needed?
Focus Areas:
  • ‚Üí Mission, vision & long-term plans
  • ‚Üí External/internal factors (tech, regulations)
  • ‚Üí Short & long-term business objectives
  • ‚Üí Organisational culture, structure & system
Data Sources:

KPIs, business plans, senior management interviews, market trend reports

2. Departmental

Task/operational level ‚Äî what does the job require?

Focus Areas:
  • ‚Üí Expected vs actual performance
  • ‚Üí KSAs needed for the job
  • ‚Üí Tools, processes, technology required
  • ‚Üí Right people in right roles
Data Sources:

Job analysis, SOPs, observation, supervisor/SME interviews

3. Individual

Person-level ‚Äî who needs what specific training?

Focus Areas:
  • ‚Üí Employee strengths & weaknesses
  • ‚Üí Career goals & learning styles
  • ‚Üí Current job performance
  • ‚Üí Future role changes
Data Sources:

Performance appraisals, skills tests, 360¬∞ feedback, coaching discussions

Data Gathering Methods

Use at least 2‚Äì3 methods together for a complete, well-rounded picture.

üí¨ Qualitative = Soft data (feelings, opinions)
üìä Quantitative = Hard data (numbers, %)

Interview (1-on-1)
Qualitative
Best for: Root cause, SMEs, personal insight
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Provides rich, detailed qualitative data
  • Consistent across interviews ‚Äî enables pattern identification
  • Can "flesh out" quantitative survey data with real examples
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Very time-consuming for the volume of data gained
  • Skewed if interviewees don't represent the target population
  • Interviewer must record ‚Äî not interpret ‚Äî responses
Focus Groups
Qualitative
Best for: Shared issues, diverse perspectives
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Develops testable hypotheses for follow-up with surveys
  • Facilitator can note nonverbal behaviours alongside statements
  • Skilled facilitation ensures all members are heard ‚Äî not just vocal ones
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Very time- and resource-intensive
  • Dominant members can give a false impression of unanimity
  • Difficult for one facilitator to run the group and take notes simultaneously
Observation
Qualitative
Best for: Physical skills, workflow, on-the-job behaviour
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Excellent for assessing psychomotor / physical skills
  • Creates standardised step-by-step procedures and job aids
  • Identifies environmental conditions that help or hinder performance
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Hard to identify exactly where a task begins and ends
  • Misses the performer's mental / cognitive processes
  • Hawthorne effect ‚Äî people behave differently when being observed
Extant Data (Records)
Quantitative
Best for: Initial baseline, org/dept level
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Hard, objective data ‚Äî not subject to interview bias
  • Reveals trends and patterns over time
  • No confidentiality issues ‚Äî data used in aggregate form
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Usually collected for purposes other than TNA ‚Äî issues must be inferred
  • No control over the methodology used to collect the data
  • May be mixed with extraneous data ‚Äî must be carefully "sifted"
Surveys & Questionnaires
Both Qual & Quant
Best for: Large groups, broad training trends
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Inexpensive, quick, and easy to administer
  • Results easy to tally ‚Äî provides both qual & quant data
  • Easy for respondents to participate at their own convenience
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Challenging to write clear questions that get the data you need
  • Respondents may check answers randomly without reading
  • Large sample needed for reliable data; answer scales can be tricky
Tests & Assessments
Quantitative
Best for: Objective KSA validation
‚úÖ Advantages
  • Highly objective and consistent ‚Äî removes interviewer bias
  • Specifically identifies the gap between current and desired K/S/A
  • Focuses training design on the precise gap, not generalised information
‚ùå Disadvantages
  • Doesn't reveal the thought processes behind a participant's answers
  • Test anxiety may cause some participants to perform poorly
  • Challenging to cover both knowledge and skills assessments in limited time
üìã Quick Reference ‚Äî When to Use Each Method
Method Use When‚Ä¶
Interview (one-to-one) Detailed personal input needed ¬∑ Interviewing managers, SMEs, key employees ¬∑ Exploring root cause of performance gaps
Focus Group Exploring shared issues or challenges ¬∑ Getting feedback from front-line staff ¬∑ Time/resources limited but diverse input needed
Observation Analysing practical skills, workflows, or on-the-job behaviour ¬∑ Roles with clear observable tasks (e.g. customer service, machine operation)
Extant Data Company has reliable data systems (HR records, KPIs, complaints) ¬∑ Best as an initial step before collecting new data
Survey/Questionnaire Broad input needed across departments ¬∑ Identifying general training trends or measuring perceptions ¬∑ Large workforce
Tests & Assessments Technical/knowledge-based roles requiring proficiency proof ¬∑ Roles where certification or accuracy is critical (IT, accounting, healthcare)
üí° Best practice: Use at least 2‚Äì3 methods for a well-rounded picture (e.g. surveys + interviews + performance data)

Key Models

McGoldrick & Tobey's 7-Step Model + Gilbert's 6-Box performance analysis.

McGoldrick & Tobey (2016)

7-Step TNA Model ‚Äî training must be needs-driven, not assumption-driven

P I S P C A R ‚Üê memorise this acronym!
1

P ‚Äî Define Purpose & Scope

Clarify why TNA is needed, what's in/out of scope, and desired outcomes.

2

I ‚Äî Gather Background Information

Review existing records, reports, and past training data before collecting new data.

3

S ‚Äî Identify Stakeholders & Gain Support

Get buy-in from executives, managers, and team leads. Credibility depends on this step.

4

P ‚Äî Plan the Needs Assessment

Choose methods, assign responsibilities, set timeline & budget.

5

C ‚Äî Collect Data

Execute the plan ‚Äî surveys, interviews, focus groups, observations.

6

A ‚Äî Analyze Data & Identify Gaps

Compare current vs desired performance. Prioritize gaps by impact and urgency.

7

R ‚Äî Report Findings & Recommend

Present actionable results (tables/charts), quick wins + long-term solutions.

üß† PISPCAR: Purpose ¬∑ Information ¬∑ Stakeholders ¬∑ Plan ¬∑ Collect ¬∑ Analyze ¬∑ Report

Human Performance Technology (HPT) Model

Rosenberg & Deterline (1992) ‚Äî a systematic process to discover performance gaps, plan improvements, and design cost-effective interventions.

6 Stages
üìä
1. Business Analysis

Understand the organisation's goals, vision, and the business context driving the performance need.

üîç
2. Performance Analysis

Identify the gap between desired and actual performance. What should be happening vs. what is happening?

üß©
3. Cause Analysis

Examine the underlying root causes of the performance gap to design comprehensive, targeted interventions.

üéØ
4. Intervention Selection

Choose the most appropriate solution ‚Äî training or non-training ‚Äî based on the root cause analysis.

üîÑ
5. Change Management

Plan and manage the transition ‚Äî communicate changes, build stakeholder support, reduce resistance.

‚úÖ
6. Evaluation

Measure whether the intervention successfully closed the performance gap and delivered business value.

Development Solutions

Once a training gap is confirmed, choose the right approach. Not every gap is solved the same way.

üí°
Not all training gaps are solved the same way. Development solutions are grouped into 4 categories ‚Äî each suited to different types of gaps, learners, and contexts. A well-designed TNA recommendation will specify which development approach is most appropriate, not just "send them to training."
üè´
1. Formal / Structured Training
Organised, scheduled programs with defined content and learning objectives
Best for: New skills, compliance, certification
üñ•Ô∏è Classroom Training

Instructor-led, face-to-face sessions. High interaction and immediate Q&A. Best when group discussion adds value.

üíª E-Learning / Online

Self-paced digital modules. Flexible, scalable, cost-effective for large groups or geographically spread teams.

üéì Workshops & Seminars

Short, focused sessions on a specific topic. Practical exercises and group activities cement learning quickly.

üì± Microlearning

Bite-sized content (3‚Äì10 mins). Reduces cognitive overload, ideal for mobile learners or refresher training.

üé≠ Simulations & Role Play

Practice real scenarios in a safe environment. Excellent for customer service, sales, and crisis response training.

üìú Blended Learning

Combines online and face-to-face delivery. Offers flexibility of e-learning with the depth of classroom interaction.

üîß
2. On-the-Job Training (OJT)
Learning by doing ‚Äî development happens within the actual work environment
Best for: Practical skills, real-world application
üëÅÔ∏è Job Shadowing

Observing an experienced employee perform their role. Low-risk introduction to new tasks or departments.

üîÑ Job Rotation

Moving employees across different roles or departments. Builds breadth of skills and cross-functional understanding.

üìã Understudy / Acting Up

Taking on a more senior role temporarily. Excellent for succession planning and leadership readiness.

üéØ Stretch Assignments

Assigning challenging projects beyond current capability. Accelerates growth through purposeful discomfort.

ü§ù Buddy System

Pairing a new employee with an experienced peer. Supports onboarding and reduces early performance gaps.

üìÅ Special Projects

Involvement in cross-functional or strategic projects. Develops collaboration, problem-solving, and leadership.

üí¨
3. Coaching & Mentoring
One-on-one guided development through dialogue, reflection, and targeted feedback
Best for: Behaviour change, leadership, personal growth
üèãÔ∏è Coaching

A structured, time-bound relationship focused on improving specific performance or closing an identified skill gap. The coach asks powerful questions to unlock the coachee's own solutions.

Key features:
‚Üí Performance-focused and goal-oriented ‚Üí Usually short-term (weeks to months) ‚Üí Coach does not need to be a subject expert ‚Üí Uses questioning, not instruction
üß≠ Mentoring

A longer-term relationship where a more experienced person guides career and personal development. The mentor shares knowledge, experience, and perspective ‚Äî not just task skills.

Key features:
‚Üí Career and development focused ‚Üí Usually long-term (months to years) ‚Üí Mentor IS a subject/domain expert ‚Üí Uses advice, guidance, and experience sharing
Coaching = fix a specific gap ¬∑ short-term ¬∑ performance | Mentoring = grow the whole person ¬∑ long-term ¬∑ career
üìö
4. Self-Directed Learning
Employee takes ownership of their own development through independent learning activities
Best for: Motivated individuals, continuous growth
üìñ Reading & Research

Books, journals, articles, and whitepapers. Low cost, flexible, and builds deep theoretical knowledge.

üåê Online Courses (MOOCs)

Platforms like Coursera, LinkedIn Learning, or Udemy. Learner chooses pace, topic, and timing.

üéß Podcasts & Webinars

Audio and live/recorded video content. Great for learning on the go and staying current with industry trends.

ü§î Reflective Practice

Journaling, after-action reviews, self-assessment. Turns everyday experience into deliberate learning.

üë• Communities of Practice

Peer learning groups, industry forums, professional associations. Learn from others facing similar challenges.

üèÜ Professional Certifications

Self-study toward industry credentials (e.g., HRD Corp, HRCI, SHRM). Validates skills with formal recognition.

üåê
5. E-Learning & Technology-Based Learning
Digital and technology-enabled learning solutions ‚Äî flexible, scalable, and trackable
Best for: Large workforce, remote teams, consistent delivery
üíª E-Learning / LMS

Online modules delivered via Learning Management Systems (LMS). Self-paced, trackable, and consistent across large groups.

‚ö° Microlearning

Short, focused learning bursts of 3‚Äì10 minutes. Addresses one specific topic. High retention, easily fits into work schedules.

üéÆ Gamification

Game elements (points, leaderboards, badges) applied to training. Boosts engagement and motivation for learners.

ü•Ω Simulations & VR

Virtual simulations and role-play scenarios. Practice in a safe environment ‚Äî ideal for sales, customer service, technical skills.

üé¨ Video-Based Learning

Instructional videos, recorded webinars, explainer animations. Visual, engaging, and easy to replay on demand.

ü§ù Blended Learning

Combines online (e-learning, microlearning) with face-to-face instruction. Gets the best of both worlds ‚Äî flexibility and human interaction.

üí° Why it's separate from Formal Training: E-Learning is technology-mediated ‚Äî it can be delivered asynchronously at scale without a live trainer, and learning progress is tracked digitally. Formal training typically requires a trainer/facilitator in real-time.
