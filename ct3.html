What is Competency-Based Training?
CBT focuses on what learners can do ‚Äî not just how long they sat in class
Definition

CBT is a training approach that focuses on developing specific skills, knowledge, and behaviours (competencies) that learners must demonstrate to perform effectively in a job or role.

Training Rationale

Training rationale = the reason for organising a training. It is identified first, based on the training needs assessment (TNA), before designing the programme.

Learner-Centric Training
Training must focus on participant needs ‚Äî not the trainer's preferences

A learner-centric programme addresses the needs of three stakeholders. Meeting all three greatly increases training success:

1. The Participants

Training is designed specifically to meet the needs of every learner in the workshop.

2. The Supervisor/Manager

The person who identified the need. Find out their expectations and incorporate them.

3. The Person Paying

The trainee, supervisor, or manager. What outcomes do they expect?

Section 2:
Learning Objectives
& Outcomes

Learning objectives are precise statements that define what learners must achieve to demonstrate the required competencies.

3 Components ¬∑ Bloom's Taxonomy ¬∑ Enabling Objectives
3 Essential Components of a Learning Objective
All objectives MUST include all three ‚Äî also known as the PCS formula
1. Performance (P)

A precise statement of what the learner will be capable of doing. Must use an action verb that is measurable. E.g., "Write a customer reply letter"

2. Condition (C)

The circumstances under which the performance occurs. Tells the trainer what resources and environment to provide. E.g., "using MS Word"

3. Standard (S)

The minimum acceptable level of performance. Can be quantity, quality, or time-based. E.g., "with no spelling mistakes"

Examples of Well-Written Objectives
Example 1

"Write a customer reply letter with no spelling mistakes by using MS Word."

P: Write a customer reply letter S: with no spelling mistakes C: using MS Word
Example 2

"Copy a table from a spreadsheet into a word processor document within three minutes and without reference to the manual."

P: Copy a table from spreadsheet into doc S: within 3 minutes C: without referencing the manual
Action Verbs vs Abstract Verbs
Always use ACTION verbs ‚Äî they are measurable. Avoid ABSTRACT verbs ‚Äî they cannot be assessed.

‚ùå Abstract Verbs (AVOID THESE)

Enabling Objectives
Sub-skills that must be learned before the Terminal Objective can be achieved

The Learning (Terminal) Objective states the final performance. Enabling Objectives are the discrete knowledge and skills needed to meet it.

Example ‚Äî Terminal Objective: "To make a pizza"
EO1: To use an oven EO2: To make pizza dough EO3: To make pizza sauce
2 questions to identify enabling objectives:
  • Why do learners need to be able to do that?
  • What do learners need to know to be able to do that?
Criteria for Writing Learning Objectives
  • Make sure there is one measurable verb in each objective
  • Each objective needs one verb ‚Äî if two verbs exist (define AND apply), mastery becomes ambiguous
  • Ensure the verb is from the highest appropriate Bloom's Taxonomy level
  • Strive to keep objectives measurable, clear and concise
  • Use only relevant terminology familiar to learners
  • Avoid double verbs like "remove and repair" ‚Äî use the more inclusive one (repair implies removing)

Section 3:
Determine Training
Content

Once learning objectives are set, structure the training content using a training plan, sequencing methods, and Gagn√©'s Nine Events of Instruction.

Training Plan ¬∑ Sequencing ¬∑ Gagn√©'s 9 Events
What is a Training Plan?
Also known as a teaching plan or session plan

A training plan is an overview/outline of how training will be implemented. It contains:

  • Training programme flow
  • Training content (topics/modules)
  • Key learning objectives
  • Duration per module
  • Assessment methods
  • Training resources/materials
Sequencing Methods
Determine the order and structure of training topics to maximise learning effectiveness
1
Job Performance Order
Learning sequence mirrors the job/task sequence.
2
Simple to Complex
Objectives sequenced by increasing complexity.
3
Critical Sequence
Topics ordered by relative importance ‚Äî most critical first.
4
Known to Unknown
Familiar topics come before unfamiliar ones.
5
Dependent Relationship
Mastery of one objective requires prior mastery of another.
6
Supportive Relationship
Transfer of learning from one objective helps another through common elements.
7
Cause to Effect
Objectives sequenced from cause to its effect.
Gagn√©'s Nine Events of Instruction
A systematic framework that aligns with how the brain processes information ‚Äî a training plan checklist
1
Gain Attention (Reception)
Use icebreakers, a problem scenario, visuals, or a thought-provoking question. Without attention, no learning happens.
2
Inform Learners of Objectives (Expectancy)
Clearly state learning objectives and link them to job relevance. Adults learn better when they know what's in it for them.
3
Stimulate Recall of Prior Learning (Retrieval)
New knowledge sticks better when connected to what learners already know. Use brainstorming, review questions, or sharing past experiences.
4
Present the Content (Selective Perception)
Deliver information in a logical, clear, and engaging way. Use a mix of methods: lecture, video, demo, storytelling, and examples.
5
Provide Learning Guidance (Semantic Encoding)
Offer strategies using job aids, frameworks, case studies, mnemonics, or visual diagrams.
6
Elicit Performance (Responding)
Learners must practise. Use role plays, group tasks, exercises, or real-world application tasks.
7
Provide Feedback (Reinforcement)
Immediate, specific, and constructive feedback helps learners correct mistakes and strengthen correct behaviour.
8
Assess Performance (Retrieval & Reinforcement)
Test whether learning objectives have been achieved. Methods: quizzes, skill demonstrations, projects, or workplace tasks.
9
Enhance Retention and Transfer (Generalisation)
Provide action plans, job aids, follow-up coaching, or workplace assignments to apply learning beyond the classroom.
Developing Test & Assessment
3 Objectives of Assessment
1

Selection

Tests used to select candidates for hiring or promotion.

2

Programme Evaluation

Determine quality of learning and impact on work performance.

3

Instructional Improvement

Tests assist in improving training quality.

Performance Assessment

Measures actual performance or job skills using the observation method. Assesses psychomotor skills like typing, repairing, or troubleshooting.

The observer can be an instructor, supervisor, experienced manager, or external expert. Performance is rated against workplace standards.

Section 4:
Determine Training
Methods

Select methods that suit the content AND the learner's learning style. Using varied methods increases engagement and effectiveness.

22 Training Methods + 7 Online Tools
22 Training Methods
A great trainer selects methods that suit both the content and the learner's learning style
1
Action-Centred Leadership
Learner leads projects and internalises leadership frameworks through real projects.
2
Action Learning
Learner applies a learning model to solve real work challenges. A coach facilitates and tracks progress.
3
Assignment
Learner given a task with guidance. May include problems, projects, or exercises to complete.
4
Brainstorming
List creative ideas about a topic. All members share ideas openly before arriving at the best option.
5
Buzz Group
Small groups each discuss a topic; a representative presents the group's consensus to the class.
6
Case Study
Learners act out roles in a written scenario and solve problems presented in the case.
7
Conference Method
12‚Äì20 people share ideas, examine facts, test assumptions, and draw agreed conclusions through open discussion.
8
Coaching Session
Trainer uses questioning and deep listening to coach the learner to solve personal or work-related issues.
9
Demonstration
Trainer shows the learner a performance of a concept, theory, or use of a machine.
10
Discovery Learning
Learning through finding principles and relationships by experimentation, trial and error.
11
Group Discussion
Large group divided into smaller groups; each discusses a specific subject and shares later.
12
Encounter Group
Unstructured group with facilitator helping participants express feelings on common issues/challenges.
13
Ice Breaker
Exercise to break down barriers among members. Goal: help everyone learn together quickly, comfortably and easily.
14
Energizer
Brief ~5 minute physical activity to increase motivation and energy, especially after a break.
15
Giving Lecture
Talk using visual aids where learners listen, take notes, and ask questions.
16
Management/Business Games
Business challenge in a game environment requiring decisions; debrief and sharing after.
17
Mirroring Technique
Learner copies the teacher to learn a skill ‚Äî dancing, tae-kwondo, singing, cooking, etc.
18
Problem Solving
Learners divided into small groups to solve a specific problem.
19
Role Play
Learners demonstrate 2 different roles in a scenario ‚Äî e.g., one is a consultant, the other a customer.
20
Triad
Groups of 3: A and B discuss; C observes and provides comments for learning purposes.
21
Simulation Exercise
Demonstrate a skill in a real-world, controlled environment ‚Äî e.g., helicopter rescue in a swimming pool.
22
Sitting Next to Nellie
New learner placed with an experienced colleague to observe work (job shadowing).

Section 5:
Review Training
Design

After completing training design, review with the business unit manager and subject matter experts before delivery.

Feedback ¬∑ Review ¬∑ Corrective Action
5.1 Gather Feedback / Comments
Conduct evaluation of the training programme BEFORE delivery by asking the right stakeholder questions

7 feedback-gathering questions to ask:

Q1

Who is in charge of training design?

Q2

How will you know if the training programme design is meeting the objective?

Q3

Who is the client that determines the training programme design?

Q4

What is the biggest expectation of the client?

Q5

Will your training programme drive the business results desired by your client?

Q6

Are you using evaluation methods to measure success?

Q7

Are there follow-up sessions planned, including assessing performance results?

5.2 Review Training Content & Sequence
  • Is the information in the content correct?
  • Has the content been edited to ensure no error?
  • Is there any important content missing?
  • Is there a smooth flow from one chapter to the next?
  • Can the participants learn well using this sequencing method?
  • Is there a break time to allow the learner to rest?
  • Have you tried the training activities?
  • Are the test and assessment relevant and practical?
